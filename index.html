My name is Anujin Ulziidelger, and my work sits at the intersection of cognitive development and cultural context. I am interested in how people think, but more specifically, how culture shapes the way thinking develops, how attention is trained, how problem-solving is taught, and how knowledge is passed on across generations.

I completed my secondary education at the 21st School of Mongolia and later at Kentridge High School in the United States. Moving between these two systems made one thing clear to me early on: learning is never culturally neutral. The same cognitive skills are valued, trained, and expressed differently depending on history, tradition, and social structure. That realization became the foundation of my academic and project-based work.

I am the Founder and President of MindMaze, an educational initiative that integrates traditional puzzle-based practices with contemporary cognitive science. Through MindMaze, I established and equipped puzzle-learning spaces in five schools, engaging more than 3,600 elementary and middle school students. These spaces use culturally rooted mechanical puzzles not as nostalgia, but as cognitive tools to strengthen executive function, spatial reasoning, and sustained attention, while reinforcing the idea that cultural heritage can actively support modern learning.

Alongside this work, I authored a research study examining the effects of puzzle-based learning on executive function and cognitive performance. My research focuses on how tactile, culturally grounded learning interventions can enhance cognitive flexibility, planning, and focus, particularly in environments where digital learning dominates but does not always engage deeply.

In recognition of this work, I was selected as a TEDx Speaker, where I spoke about preserving cultural heritage through education and why cognition develops most meaningfully when learning extends beyond formal classrooms. My talk is expected to be published on the official TED Talks YouTube channel.

Beyond research, I am active in youth advocacy and international education. Through the East Asian Youth Network, I represented over 75,000 youth from East Asian Red Cross communities and moderated in-person youth conferences in Macau and Mongolia. In 2023, I was selected as a FLEX Capital City Representative and served as a Mongolian delegate at the U.S. Department of State‚Äôs Civic Education Selective Workshop, experiences that further shaped my interest in civic learning across cultures.

I am also an athlete. I competed as a varsity golf player at Kentridge High School and currently serve as a board member of the Mongolian Golf Association. Sports have reinforced my belief that cognition is not confined to classrooms; discipline, focus, and strategic thinking are learned through embodied experience as much as academic study.
